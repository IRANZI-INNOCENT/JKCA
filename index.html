Executive Summary

IMARISHA Enterprise is a refugee-led social enterprise designed to transform the skills of artisans in Kakuma and Kalobeyei Refugee Camps into a sustainable source of income. By establishing a collaborative fashion hub, delivering targeted skill-building programs, and launching a hybrid sales ecosystem, we aim to empower 2,000 refugee artisans by 2030, with a special focus on teenage mothers.

Our vibrant shukas, modern dresses, bedsheets, sofa covers, and accessories connect refugee craftsmanship with markets in Kakuma, Nairobi, and beyond through our physical shop and SokoMoja e-commerce platform, fostering economic independence and community resilience.

Project Overview

IMARISHA Enterprise tackles economic isolation, limited market access, and lack of production resources faced by refugee artisans. Through our integrated approach, we create pathways for artisans to transform their traditional skills into sustainable livelihoods.

Market Context

Kakuma/Kalobeyei Context

Demographics and Challenges

Kakuma and Kalobeyei Refugee Camps host over 280,000 refugees from South Sudan, Somalia, DR Congo, and beyond (UNHCR 2024), facing severe economic constraints:

  • Youth (60% of population) and women face highest unemployment
  • Restricted work permits limit economic opportunities
  • 30% cut in WFP food rations since 2023
  • Artisans lack production tools and market access
  • Kenya's 6.5% inflation (2025) erodes purchasing power

Opportunities

  • 85% mobile phone penetration enables digital platforms
  • Kenya's apparel market growing 10% annually (KSh 50B in 2025)
  • Rich cultural traditions create unique product potential
  • Growing demand for authenticity and ethical products

Kenya Context

Nairobi has emerged as a fashion hub, with young professionals (25–35 years) and retailers embracing sustainable, artisanal products. The city's KSh 110 billion tech ecosystem, powered by M-Pesa's KSh 31 trillion in annual transactions, enables scalable e-commerce solutions.

Kenya's Vision 2030 and MSME policies encourage inclusive businesses, creating pathways for refugee products to reach urban markets and, eventually, East Africa.

Emerging demand for "Made in Africa" goods positions IMARISHA to tap into regional trade networks, starting with Uganda and Ethiopia, where similar cultural products thrive.

Market Gap

Refugee artisans are excluded from mainstream fashion supply chains due to geographic isolation, lack of production infrastructure, and limited digital marketing skills. Existing CBOs and NGOs offer basic training (e.g., sewing) but rarely connect artisans to viable markets or provide advanced tools. IMARISHA bridges this gap with a tech-savvy, market-driven model that prioritizes quality, accessibility, and storytelling.

Core Features and Functionalities

IMARISHA integrates a physical fashion hub in Kakuma with the SokoMoja e-commerce platform, organized into three pillars—Production Empowerment, Market Access, and Skill Development—to maximize impact. Each feature is designed to address specific challenges faced by refugee artisans.

1 Production Empowerment

Provide artisans with a fully equipped workspace, materials, and fair compensation to scale production of market-ready fashion products.

Fashion Production Hub

A 200-square-meter center equipped with sewing machines, looms, cutting tables, and storage for raw materials. Artisans collaborate to produce shukas, dresses, bedsheets, sofa covers, and accessories.

Operation: Mon-Sat, 8AM-5PM, rotating shifts for 30 artisans
Impact: 500 items/month (tripling current output)
Tech: Inventory tracking system with dashboard

Skill Purchase Model

Artisans are paid per item or hourly for complex designs, with contracts signed via a mobile app. Payments are disbursed weekly through M-Pesa, with a digital ledger recording earnings.

Operation: Clear contracts with deliverables
Impact: KSh 5,000-10,000/month per artisan
Tech: M-Pesa integration with secure authentication

Quality Assurance System

Products undergo checks for stitching strength, color consistency, and design accuracy. Verified items receive a digital "IMARISHA Certified" badge on SokoMoja.

Operation: Daily checks with 90% pass target
Impact: 15% sales boost, returns under 5%
Tech: Digital verification badges and scorecards

2 Market Access

Connect artisan products to diverse buyers through a physical shop, e-commerce, and strategic partnerships, maximizing reach and revenue.

SokoMoja E-Commerce Platform

A Progressive Web App where artisans list products with high-resolution photos, personal stories, and tags. Buyers can filter by price, category, or location. AI suggests trending items.

Operation: Simplified Swahili listing form, M-Pesa/card payments
Impact: 2,000 buyers by year 2, KSh 1M/month revenue
Tech: Django Oscar, Hugging Face NLP, optimized for 2G

Physical Shop in Kakuma

A 50-square-meter shop near Kakuma's main market sells products with artisan stories. A tablet-based POS system records sales and syncs with SokoMoja.

Operation: Daily 9AM-6PM, staffed by trained artisans
Impact: 500 buyers/month, KSh 200,000/month revenue
Tech: POS app with QR code linking to online listings

Nairobi Market Linkages

Partnerships with boutiques sell IMARISHA products on consignment. Pop-up stalls at markets and fashion showcases target young professionals.

Operation: Nairobi coordinator manages deliveries
Impact: 1,000 buyers/year, 30% income increase
Tech: CRM system for partner tracking

Regional Trade Hub

Simplifies exports with tariff calculator and multi-currency wallet. Lists products for East African buyers with guides on packaging and customs.

Operation: Targets retailers in Kampala and Addis Ababa
Impact: 100 exports/month by year 3, KSh 100,000/month
Tech: Blockchain for transparent transactions

3 Skill Development

Equip artisans and youth with advanced craftsmanship and business skills to enhance products and foster entrepreneurship.

Training Academy

Offers courses on tailoring, design, marketing, and bookkeeping, with Swahili video tutorials and hands-on workshops. Free for teenage mothers.

Operation: 2 sessions/month for 20 learners each
Impact: 100 artisans trained/year, 10% start businesses
Tech: Mobile-friendly LMS with video integration

Peer Mentorship Chat

Connects artisans to mentors via a WhatsApp-integrated chatbot. Users can book sessions, ask questions, and receive personalized guidance.

Operation: Top artisans earn extra as mentors
Impact: Upskills 50 youth in year 1, 20% launch side hustles
Tech: Rasa chatbot with WhatsApp integration

Community Skill-Sharing Forum

A digital forum where artisans post tips, ask questions, or crowdfund tools. Weekly prompts encourage knowledge sharing.

Operation: Accessible via SokoMoja, moderated for quality
Impact: Reduces material costs by 5% through collaboration
Tech: Real-time forum with mobile optimization

Additional Features

Offline-First Access

Caches content for 2G networks and offers USSD (*123#) for non-smartphone users to browse and order products.

Multi-Language UX

Swahili and English interface, with plans for Dinka and Somali by year 2, making the platform accessible to diverse refugee groups.

Artisan Dashboard

Shows earnings, pending orders, training progress, and quality scores, empowering artisans to manage their work.

Admin Dashboard

Tracks sales, payouts, inventory, and quality metrics, allowing efficient management of the enterprise.

Challenges and Mitigations

Market Access Barriers

Challenge:

Kakuma's isolation limits buyer reach, with only 10% of residents accessing external markets.

Mitigation:

  • Use NGO networks (e.g., IRC's 5,000 monthly visitors) to promote shop
  • Drive online traffic with KSh 10,000/month social ads, targeting Nairobi's 2 million Instagram users
  • Develop USSD platform for non-smartphone users to browse and order products

Artisan Skill Gaps

Challenge:

Inconsistent stitching or design skills lead to 20% product rejections initially.

Mitigation:

  • Implement 2-week onboarding with daily quality feedback, reducing rejections to 5%
  • Pair new artisans with mentors for 1 month of guided training
  • Create standardized patterns and templates to ensure consistency

Funding Constraints

Challenge:

KSh 300,000 covers only basic setup, risking delays if grants fail.

Mitigation:

  • Bootstrap SokoMoja using existing Django skills (KSh 0 dev cost)
  • Sell 100 shukas in month 1 (KSh 50,000) to fund materials
  • Apply to 3 grants by month 2 to diversify funding sources

Competition

Challenge:

Local tailors and global brands offer similar products at KSh 1,500-10,000, undercutting IMARISHA's KSh 2,000-5,000 range.

Mitigation:

  • Differentiate with "Made in Kakuma" storytelling, targeting ethical buyers willing to pay a 10% premium
  • Offer bundles (e.g., shuka + bag for KSh 3,000) to compete locally
  • Focus on unique designs that blend traditional and modern elements
